Mental models are representations of how the world works. They're the lenses you use to interpret situations, make decisions, and predict outcomes.

The goal isn't to have one perfect model — it's to have a rich collection so you can pick the right lens for the situation.

## My most-used models

**First principles** — Strip away assumptions. What is actually true here? What must be true for this to work? Separate facts from conventions.

**Inversion** — Think about problems backwards. Instead of "how do I succeed," ask "what would guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things.

**Second-order effects** — Every action has direct and indirect consequences. The indirect ones often dominate over time. Ask: "and then what?"

**Opportunity cost** — Every choice is also a set of unchosen alternatives. The real cost of any decision includes what you gave up.

**Margin of safety** — Build in buffers. The world is uncertain. Things take longer and cost more than you think. Plan for it.

## The meta-model

The best mental model is one you can actually apply today. An elegant framework you can't use is just trivia.

Test every model you learn: "In what situation would I use this? What would I do differently because of it?"
