An agentic organization is one built around the assumption that intelligence is abundant and coordination is scarce.

This is the opposite of how most companies are built today, where intelligence (human time and attention) is the primary constraint.

## What it implies

**Fewer humans, higher leverage.** Each person in an agentic org is a system architect, not an executor. Their job is to design and steward workflows, not run them.

**Memory infrastructure matters.** In a human org, context lives in people's heads. People leave, and the context goes with them. Agentic orgs need explicit memory systems — documented decisions, structured context, institutional knowledge that outlives any individual.

**Speed compounds.** If you can run 10x more experiments with the same team, you learn 10x faster. The compounding effect of this over 2-3 years is enormous.

## The coordination problem

Agents are bad at coordination with other agents unless the interfaces are designed carefully. This is where humans stay essential — not as doers, but as designers.

Who decides what the agents work on? Who handles the edge cases? Who monitors for failure modes?

These are human jobs for the foreseeable future.

## Where we're at

Most "AI-first" companies are actually just human companies with AI tools. They've automated some tasks but haven't changed the organizational structure.

True agentic orgs are rare. But they're coming. And they'll have an operating advantage that looks like an unfair one.
