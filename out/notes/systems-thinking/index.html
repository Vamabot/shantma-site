Systems Thinking

February 20, 2026

Systems thinking is the practice of understanding how things influence each other within a whole, rather than analyzing each component in isolation.

Core insight

Every complex outcome is the result of a system — a set of elements, interconnections, and a function or purpose. To change outcomes, you usually need to change the system, not just the elements.

Key concepts

Feedback loops — Outputs become inputs. This is how systems self-regulate (or spiral out of control). Reinforcing loops amplify change. Balancing loops resist change.

Delays — There's almost always a gap between action and result. This is the source of most overshooting and undershooting. When you don't account for delays, you intervene too hard, too late.

Leverage points — Places in a system where small interventions produce large changes. The highest leverage is usually at the level of goals, information flows, and rules — not at the level of individual components.

Stocks and flows — Stocks are the quantities in a system (people, money, knowledge). Flows are what changes the stocks (births/deaths, revenue/costs, learning/forgetting).

Why it matters for building

Most problems that look like people problems are actually system problems. The individual is behaving rationally given the incentives and information available to them.

If you fix the person but not the system, the system will produce the same behavior through the next person.
