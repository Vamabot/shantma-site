On Building With Purpose

December 10, 2025

Most people build to make money. A few build because they can't not. The difference shows up everywhere.

There's a version of building that's essentially sophisticated gambling. You pick a sector, identify an opportunity, build a thing, try to exit. The goal is the exit.

Then there's a different version. You build because you see something that doesn't exist and shouldn't not exist. The outcome you want is the thing being built — the money is just evidence that you got it right.

I've done both. The second one is harder and more worth it.

The tell

You can identify which mode someone is in by how they talk about failure.

The first type treats failure as loss. There was a prize to be won, and now there isn't.

The second type treats failure as information. The thing they wanted to exist still doesn't exist. They're closer to knowing why. They'll try again.

It's not that the second type doesn't care about money. They do — money is leverage, and leverage is how you do more of the work that matters. But the money is downstream of the thing.

Purpose is a filter

Having a clear purpose is mostly valuable because of what it lets you say no to.

The hardest moments in building aren't the hard technical problems or the fundraising. They're the forks where the right answer for building the thing you care about and the right answer for optimizing metrics point in different directions.

If you don't have a purpose, you'll optimize the metrics. And then a few years later you'll have a successful-looking thing that doesn't mean anything.

Purpose is the filter you run every major decision through. Not every decision — that's exhausting and leads to paralysis. But every major one.

People are the purpose

I've landed on something simple as a north star: people are the purpose.

Every business worth building is ultimately in service of people — the ones who use it, work in it, or are affected by it. Not abstractly. Specifically. Faces you can picture.

When I lose the thread on why something matters, I try to find my way back to the specific people. Who benefits? How does their life change? Is that real or is it a story I'm telling myself?

That last question is the important one. It's easy to construct narratives about impact. It's harder to stay honest about whether you're building something that actually helps real people or just something that looks like it does.

Building things that last

Short-term thinking is an addiction. It feels productive because it generates quick feedback — metrics, revenue, attention. But it compounds badly.

The things worth building have long time horizons. Relationships built over years. Trust earned slowly. Products that solve hard problems instead of easy ones.

The best founders I know play long games with long-term people. They're patient about outcomes and impatient about learning. They run fewer experiments but go deeper on each one.

That's the orientation I'm trying to hold. Not always successfully. But the north star is clear.

Build things that matter. For people who are real. With people you trust. Over a time horizon that lets the compound interest work.

That's it.
